## Brainstorming Outcomes

### Bottleneck Identified
The talent pipeline's biggest leak is **Stage 2: training to first impactful role/project**. Plenty of people are interested and trained (7,000+ BlueDot alumni). But the transition to doing impactful work is blocked by:
- Orgs need senior, autonomous contributors (Teichman study, March 2026)
- 10-30% of fellowship alumni cycle into another fellowship rather than converting to work (Clay dataset, 600+ alumni)
- High-talent people don't apply for funding because the process feels opaque and high-friction
- High-talent people don't know which opportunities exist or which path (role, startup, funded project) is right for them

### Concept: Curated Talent Marketplace
**Monthly in-person event in SF (~3 hours, ~50 people)**

**One-liner:** Create the highest-signal talent marketplace in AI safety, where every attendee leaves with at least one concrete opportunity they wouldn't have found otherwise.

**The core insight:** The curation IS the product. What makes this valuable isn't the format, it's that BlueDot guarantees everyone in the room is exceptional. That's the promise to both sides:
- To talent: "Every person you meet here is worth your time."
- To institutions: "We've done the screening. These are the people you should know about but don't."

**Three types of valuable interactions, all in one room:**
1. **Talent meets institutions** - hiring leads, funding conversations, program placements
2. **Talent meets talent** - co-founder matching, collaboration opportunities, peer connections
3. **Institutions meet institutions** - coordination between funders, orgs, and programs (also underserved; these institutions aren't fully coordinated and some don't know about each other)

**Two sides of the room:**
- **Side A (10-15): Institutional leaders.** Fund managers (Coefficient Giving, SFF), program directors (MATS, Apart, Constellation), hiring managers at safety orgs. The demand side.
- **Side B (30-35): Top-tier talent.** BlueDot's best pipeline output + external talent pools (MATS alumni, ATK, etc.). People who could do impactful work but haven't found their specific path yet.

**BlueDot's unique edge:** Nobody else has a curated pipeline of 7,000+ alumni with data on who's most promising, AND relationships with institutional leaders. They can curate both sides of the room.

**Key differentiator from EAG:** Exclusivity. EAG has a mix of quality levels. This is top talent only. Not watered down. A more exclusive, higher-signal version of EAG's matching function, compressed into 3 hours monthly.

**Differentiation:**
- vs. EAG: Smaller, more exclusive, higher signal-to-noise, specific career-acceleration outcome
- vs. Incubator Week: Lighter (3h not 5 days), broader (50 not 7), recurring (monthly), earlier-stage (direction-finding not pitch-ready). Serves as feeder pipeline for Incubator Week.
- vs. Hackathons: Not output-focused, about matching people to opportunities
- vs. Mox socials: Curated attendance, structured format, specific outcome tracking

**Potential add-ons:**
- Tie to BlueDot Rapid Grants (same-day micro-funding for strongest ideas)
- Serve as feeder pipeline for Incubator Week
- Lower the activation energy for funding applications (builders get real-time signal from funders on what excites them)

**Measurement (two waves):**
- **Same-day:** connections made, follow-up meetings scheduled, interest signals
- **30/60/90 days:** applications submitted, interviews conducted, offers made, projects funded, collaborations started

### Key strategic arguments (for the submission)
- BlueDot's thesis: focus on the top 1% of talent and get them into impactful work
- Coefficient Giving (March 2026): capacity-building work is undervalued relative to its impact, and they have more funding appetite than good applications
- The funding application barrier: talented people have a high bar for applying. This event lowers friction by giving them face-to-face access to funders before formal applications.
- Institutional coordination gap: safety orgs, funders, and programs don't coordinate enough. Putting their leaders in the same room monthly creates ongoing coordination infrastructure.
