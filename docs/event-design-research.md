## 1. Apart Research Sprint Model

### Format and schedule
Apart runs **monthly, 3-day research sprints (hackathons)**, hybrid: online via Discord plus satellite in-person locations worldwide. Typically Friday through Sunday. Each focuses on a specific AI safety topic chosen with a co-hosting partner.

**What Apart provides centrally:**
- Topic framing and background materials
- Keynote speakers and live-streamed talks
- Online submission platform with daily email updates
- Expert judging panel for feedback
- Graphics and promo materials for local organizers
- Q&A and expert mentorship on Discord throughout
- Prizes (typically $2,000 total: $1,000 / $500 / $300 / $100 / $100)
- Post-hackathon pipeline into the Apart Lab fellowship for top participants

### Satellite event model
The key to scalability. Local organizers just provide a venue and gather people; Apart handles everything else. Active satellite locations at Georgia Tech, AI Alignment Bangalore, CEEALAR (UK), EA Denmark, NIT Agartala, and others across multiple continents. Barrier to hosting is very low: you need a room and some people.

- Source: [Apart hackathon guide](https://apartresearch.com/news/how-to-organize-a-research-hackathon), [Satellite locations](https://apartresearch.com/sprints/locations)

### Scaling trajectory
| Event | Signups | Entries |
|-------|---------|---------|
| AI Security Evaluation (May 2024) | 118 | 12 |
| AI Safety Entrepreneurship (Jan 2025) | 225 | 18 |
| Women in AI Safety (Mar 2025) | 350 | 30 |
| AI Control (Mar 2026, with Redwood) | 766 | 126 |

**Cumulative (2025):** 3,500+ participants across 42 research sprints, 485 research reports submitted.

**Growth drivers:** (a) co-hosting with increasingly prominent partners, (b) satellite location model creating local buy-in, (c) consistent monthly cadence building community habit, (d) clear pipeline from hackathon to fellowship to publication.

- Source: [Apart sprints](https://apartresearch.com/sprints/ai-control-hackathon-2026-03-20-to-2026-03-22), [2025 fundraiser](https://apartresearch.com/donate/2025)

### Co-hosting approach
Apart regularly co-hosts with established AI safety orgs. The pattern: partner provides research agenda and expert judges; Apart provides event infrastructure, community, and talent pipeline.

| Partner | Event | Special |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Redwood Research | AI Control Hackathon (2025, 2026) | 1st place gets funded trip to ControlConf |
| BlueDot Impact | AIxBio Hackathon (Apr 2026) | DNA synthesis screening, pandemic early warning |
| METR | Code Red Hackathon (2024) | Generated 230 eval ideas, 108 specs, 28 task implementations |
| Cooperative AI Foundation | Multi-agent safety sprint | Fed into a research report |
| Goodfire | Interpretability hackathons | Used Goodfire's Ember API |

- Source: [Apart impact](https://apartresearch.com/impact/testimonials), [Manifund page](https://manifund.org/projects/apart-research-research-and-talent-acceleration)

### Outcomes
- **22 peer-reviewed publications** (including 2 ICLR 2025 orals) in NeurIPS, ACL, ICLR
- **Apart Lab fellowship**: top participants get mentored research with project management, compute, and publication support. 10 new fellows from Code Red alone; 36 new fellows in a recent 3-month period.
- **Direct utility for partners**: METR got usable evaluation task implementations
- **Career pipeline**: participants demonstrate research ability to AI safety employers

- Source: [Apart impact report](https://apartresearch.com/impact/report)

## 2. ControlConf and AI Safety Conferences

### ControlConf 2026 (Redwood Research + FAR.AI)
Two-day conference on AI control, **April 18-19, 2026 in Berkeley, CA**. Pre-conference **Control Workshop on April 17** for newcomers.

**ControlConf 2025** (first edition, Feb 2025): Talks by Tim Hua (hierarchical monitoring), Owain Evans (deluding AIs), Neel Nanda (white-box control), Vivek Hebbar (low-stakes control).

**The Apart connection:** AI Control Hackathon runs a month before ControlConf; hackathon winner gets funded trip. This creates a hackathon-to-conference funnel.

- Source: [ControlConf](https://controlconf.org/), [Redwood blog](https://blog.redwoodresearch.org/p/announcing-controlconf-2026), [FAR.AI](https://far.ai/events/event-list/berkeley-controlconf-2026)

### AI Safety Unconference (Melbourne)
Ran in 2023 and 2025 (AISUM). Published retrospective from 2023.

**Format:**
- One-day, ~9:30am-3:30pm
- Attendees pitch sessions, everyone votes, organizers build schedule via constrained optimization
- Morning: registration, lightning talks, pitches, voting
- Afternoon: 3-4 parallel sessions with breaks
- "201+" level (not introductory)
- Application-based

**Key retrospective takeaway:** Unconference format works well for communities with shared context. Surfaces what people actually want to discuss vs. what organizers guess. Low-cost to organize (main work is logistics, not content curation).

- Source: [AISUM](https://aisum25.com/), [EA Forum retrospective](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/rBteAqbfqvaFMvpv5/what-we-learned-from-running-an-australian-ai-safety)

- Session types: debates, storytelling, workshops, talks, "memo sessions" (author writes 2-6 page essay, small group reads then Q&A), music/jams
- Strong emphasis on informal interaction: fireside conversations, dance parties, late-night discussions

**Manifest** runs the following weekend (June 12-14) at same venue. Prediction markets and forecasting focus. Together, LessOnline + Manifest bring 600+ people each summer.

## 3. EAG Events and 1-on-1 Scheduling

### Format and scale
EAG (Effective Altruism Global) conferences are weekend-long events (Friday-Sunday) run by CEA. The core value proposition is structured 1-on-1 networking, not talks. Application-only.

| Format | Scale | Cost/attendee |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| **EAG** (flagship) | 800-1,500+ attendees. SF, London. Selective admissions. | ~$1,200 |
| **EAGx** (community-organized) | 180-600 attendees. Regional. | $300-600 |
| **EA Connect** (newer) | 3,000+ registrations (Dec 2025), 1,500+ newcomers. | Lower |
| **EA Summits** (pilot) | Introductory events for regions without EAGx. | Lower |

- Source: [EAGxAmsterdam retro](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/hgmEvDu25uDCcrLGx/eagxamsterdam-2025-what-we-learned), [EAGxCDMX retro](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/HYcvbnoKNahFZ2ZMW/eagxcdmx-2025-retrospective)

### How the 1-on-1 scheduling system works
1. **Pre-event profiles**: Attendees create Swapcard profiles with role, org, cause areas, what they're looking for, what they can offer.
2. **Browse and match**: Browse other attendees. App surfaces AI-based recommendations.
3. **Request meetings**: Send "Meet" requests with a short message explaining why. Propose available time slots.
4. **Accept/decline**: Accepted meetings get time + location (specific numbered tables in designated areas).
5. **During the event**: 30-minute 1-on-1 slots. Power users book 10-15 per day. Physical "1-on-1 area" with numbered tables.
6. **The culture**: Attendees are explicitly encouraged to make 1-on-1s the primary activity, not talks. CEA provides coaching on good 1-on-1s.

**Key metrics:** EAGx events target ~10 new connections per attendee. EAGxAmsterdam 2025 hit that target. EAGxCDMX 2025: ~1,714 total new connections across 182 attendees (~9.4 per person). Cost per connection: ~$70 (EAGx) to ~$130 (flagship EAG).

- Source: [Networking at EAG guide](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/tWeikZNJohALmmWtx/networking-at-eag-x-how-to-get-the-most-value-from), [CEA 1-on-1 guidance](https://www.centreforeffectivealtruism.org/community-health/resources/avoiding-some-1-1-pitfalls-at-ea-global-x), [Improving 1-on-1s](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/uvmB5RqSMhhvL6XMA/ea-global-we-re-improving-1-on-1s)

### What makes EAG high-impact
- **Career pivots and co-founder matching**: "One of the most important factors in starting a high-impact charity is the right co-founder. EAG has been a place where I consistently met some of the most promising candidates."
- **Density of relevant people**: Application filter means most attendees share baseline values and seriousness.
- **Actionable conversations**: Best 1-on-1s aren't "tell me about yourself" but "I'm trying to do X, can you help with Y." Nina Friedrich's guide: "Conversations don't generate impact by themselves, actions do."

### Common criticisms and failure modes
1. **Too many 1-on-1s / exhaustion**: 10-15 meetings/day is "extreme" (calebp, EA Forum). Preference for ~5/day with half booked spontaneously, leaving room for small groups and serendipity.
2. **Transactional / instrumentalizing feel**: The "what can you do for me" dynamic is a recurring complaint.
3. **Pre-booking pressure**: Calendars fill up early. Late joiners and newcomers miss out.
4. **Small group conversations are hard**: Swapcard handles 1-on-1s but makes 3-5 person discussions difficult.
5. **Imposter syndrome**: First-timers report feeling overwhelmed.
6. **No-shows and scheduling friction**: CEA guidance asks people to accept/decline promptly.

- Source: [calebp EAG NYC](https://forum-bots.effectivealtruism.org/posts/LrxLa9jfaNcEzqex3/calebp-s-shortform?commentId=9G5SqLKjzijiafs7u), [EAG London reflections](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/KAhETmcWuH75sxaTa/reflections-following-eag-london-conference-strategy-and-in), [First EAG feelings](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/xFgwv2JcCusBvMiHD/my-first-eag-a-mix-of-feelings)

### The small event model (most relevant to your case)
**EA North 2025** in Sheffield: 35 people, one day, GBP 1,765 total (GBP 50/attendee, GBP 11/connection). Included talks, 1-on-1s, career workshop, cause-area meetups. Organizer had no event background, did it in ~30 hours alongside a full-time job. Attendees rated 8.8/10, 4.56 new connections on average. Conclusion: **communities should run more 25-75 person regional events.** All templates shared freely.

- Source: [EA North retro](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/m9sTFoAsE8dSnzoBt/12x-more-cost-effective-than-eag-how-i-organised-ea-north) -- "12x more cost-effective than EAG"

## 4. Swapcard and Event Matching Platforms

### Swapcard core features
- **Attendee profiles**: Customizable with goals, focus areas, interests.
- **AI matchmaking**: Recommends people based on profile data, behavior, roles. Refines in real-time.
- **1-on-1 scheduling**: Search, request, accept/decline. Conflict detection with sessions.
- **Smart Meeting Generator** (paid add-on): AI auto-schedules meetings between groups (speed-networking, hosted buyer programs).
- **Analytics**: Connection counts, engagement metrics, session attendance.
- **Virtual/hybrid**: Webinars, streaming, virtual networking.
- **SOC 2 Type 2 and GDPR compliant.**

### How EAG uses Swapcard
After acceptance, attendees get access weeks before the event. Fill out profiles, browse others, send "Meet" requests with personalized messages. App suggests AI matches. During event, shows schedule, meeting locations (table numbers), last-minute changes. EA Connect 2025 used a "Collaboration Path" feature.

**Limitation in EA context:** Handles 1-on-1s well but makes small group conversations (3-5 people) "extremely hard to organise."

### Pricing
- **Starter**: Up to 200 attendees/year. ~$610/year. Self-service.
- **Professional**: Up to 1,000 attendees/year. Custom pricing.
- **Enterprise**: Unlimited. White-label, onsite support. Custom.
- **Add-ons extra**: Smart Meeting Generator, branded app, lead capture, floor plans.

**For a 50-person monthly event, Swapcard is overkill.** Designed for large trade shows.

- Source: [Swapcard pricing](https://www.swapcard.com/pricing-plans), [Swapcard features](https://www.swapcard.com/features/event-networking)

### Competitor landscape

| Platform | Differentiator | AI Features | Best For |
|----------|---------------|-------------|----------|
| **Swapcard** | Most features (117), strongest AI | 13 AI features | Large conferences, trade shows |
| **Brella** | Simpler UI, strong matchmaking | Intent-based matching | Mid-size B2B events |
| **Grip** | Lighter, fewer features (40) | 2 AI features | Simpler event networking |
| **Whova** | All-in-one, community features | Moderate | Multi-format events |
| **b2match** | Meeting-focused, hosted buyer | Meeting scheduling | B2B matchmaking |
| **Remo** | Virtual-first, visual floor plan | Virtual networking AI | Virtual/hybrid events |

- Source: [Swapcard vs Grip](https://saasipedia.com/compare/grip-vs-swapcard), [Gateway to Events review](https://gatewaytoevents.com/tools/swapcard)

### What you can replicate with simpler tools (for 50 people / 3 hours)

| EAG Element | Simple Replacement |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Profile creation + browsing** | Airtable form (intake) with shared view so attendees browse each other pre-event. Fields: name, role, org, "looking for," "can offer," topics. |
| **Matching/recommendations** | For 50 people, manual curation or a simple matching script. Airtable automation or n8n workflow suggests 3-5 matches per person, emailed before the event. |
| **Meeting scheduling** | Pre-assign 3-4 structured 20-min 1-on-1 slots based on matching, plus free time. Or a simple Google Sheet grid (time slots x table numbers). Or host-facilitated "speed dating" rounds. |
| **RSVP and event page** | Luma (free for small events). Zapier integration with Swapcard exists if you ever need to bridge. |
| **Follow-up** | Post-event email via Luma or Airtable automation. Feedback form + "who do you want an intro to" field. |

**The EA North model is closest to your use case:** 35 people, minimal tooling, GBP 50/person, 4.5 connections per attendee. Basic forms and spreadsheets, no Swapcard.

- Source: [Luma-Swapcard Zapier](https://zapier.com/apps/luma/integrations/swapcard-organizer/)

## 5. AI Safety Talent Pipeline Bottlenecks

### The core problem: senior talent scarcity creates a cascade

The primary bottleneck is not a shortage of interested people, but a shortage of **senior, independent contributors who can hit the ground running**. Without enough senior people, orgs can't mentor juniors, which blocks the entire pipeline.

### Evidence from multiple sources

**BlueDot Impact (Li-Lian Ang, Feb 2026):** After interviewing 10+ hiring managers, the key challenge: "sourcing talent who can own and lead work independently, and care deeply about AI risk." They want people with strong track records, through professional experience or impressive projects.
- Source: [Hiring needs blog](https://blog.bluedot.org/p/we-asked-ai-safety-orgs-about-their-hiring-needs)

**MATS Research / Teichman study (March 2026):** Based on 23 interviews with hiring managers, research leads, and funders: "AI safety organizations are capacity constrained by a lack of senior researchers who can mentor and supervise junior talent. This bottleneck drives hyper-selective hiring, blocks promising candidates, and is being exacerbated by AI automation, which is raising the bar for human contribution rather than lowering it." Six actions recommended for field-builders, including standardized mentor evaluations, direct policy embedding, and production codebase access. Also identifies an "Automation Paradox" where AI tools raise the bar for what humans must contribute.
- Source: [EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/jwwrC4n9H53doRjRH/ai-safety-talent-needs-in-2026-insights-for-field-building)

**80,000 Hours survey (Sept 2025):** 38 AI safety org leaders surveyed.
- Best upskilling paths (by frequency): BlueDot courses, Talos Fellowship, MATS, GovAI fellowships, Cambridge ERA:AI, Pivotal Research Fellowship
- For non-technical roles: BlueDot course + 1-3 years relevant experience can be sufficient
- Strongest advice: "Publish your outputs" and "attend events and get to know people"
- AI governance/policy now ranked #1 career path (above technical safety research)
- Source: [80,000 Hours](https://80000hours.org/2025/09/survey-results-what-ai-safety-orgs-want-in-a-hire/)

### Where the pipeline leaks

**Stage 1 (Interest to Training): Relatively healthy.** BlueDot has trained 7,000+. MATS runs regular cohorts. TARA (Technical Alignment Research Accelerator) targets people who can't relocate, with a 14-week part-time format, 90% completion, 9.43/10 satisfaction.
- Source: [TARA EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/p4ekwamhsw6jpKEcu/widening-ai-safety-s-talent-pipeline-by-meeting-people-where)

**Stage 2 (Training to First Role): THE BIG LEAK.** Chris Clay's analysis of 600+ alumni from 9 major AI safety fellowships: over 10% did another fellowship after completing one, suggesting they couldn't convert to jobs. For ERA and Talos Network, this "fellowship-to-fellowship" rate was nearly 30%. On the positive side, ~80% of alumni remain in AI safety in some capacity.
- Source: [EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/SJBBgupFx7SXyBj2B/where-do-ai-safety-fellows-go-analyzing-a-dataset-of-600)

**MATS alumni data (Oct 2024):** Of 72 surveyed alumni (first four cohorts): 78% described current work as alignment-related. 54% applied and advanced past first round. Of those who shared details, 64% accepted an offer. 68% published alignment research during/after MATS. 78% said publication "possibly" or "probably" wouldn't have happened without MATS.
- Source: [MATS impact analysis](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/kJA9q3SGycx6TXjcF/mats-alumni-impact-analysis)

**Stage 3 (Hired but Not Effective): Less data.** The Teichman study flags orgs want "Connector/Iterator" hybrid profiles, people who combine research taste with ability to set new agendas. Pure iteration skills increasingly automated.

### Coefficient Giving / Asya Bergal (March 2026)
Many marginal hires at larger AI safety orgs would have more impact founding or joining capacity-building organizations. The bottleneck is not just "more researchers" but building the infrastructure that produces researchers at scale. Capacity-building work is currently undervalued relative to its impact.
- Source: [EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/rAqKSSXankvys2Fzu/the-case-for-ai-safety-capacity-building-work)

### Summary: where the bottleneck actually is
**The answer is primarily "interested people can't get hired," with a twist.** Plenty of people are interested. Orgs need senior, autonomous contributors, and most pipeline entrants are junior. The system lacks mentorship capacity to develop juniors into seniors. AI automation makes this worse by raising the bar. Fellowship-to-fellowship cycling suggests many people train without converting to employment.

## 6. Structured Career-Matching Event Formats

### YC Demo Day: the "3-minute pitch" model
200+ companies present to ~1,000 invite-only investors. Each gets a 3-minute pitch (standardized: analysis of 87 pitches found same 8 elements in every successful one). Billions in commitments within 48 hours. Power comes from: extreme curation, time compression, pre-existing trust.

**Adaptation:** A "Talent Demo Day" where 8-10 pre-selected candidates give 3-minute presentations about skills, projects, and goals, followed by structured matching with org reps.
- Source: [YC Demo Day FAQ](https://ycombinator.com/demoday/faq)

### HackerX: speed-recruiting events
500+ invite-only events in 175+ cities. Pre-vetting on both sides: every developer reviewed, every company curated. Speed-interview sessions: candidates rotate through brief conversations with up to 20 companies in 2 hours. Typical: 120-150 candidates, structured schedule. ~$595/employer.

**Key design principle:** Pre-vetting creates high signal-to-noise. Candidates know companies are actually hiring; companies know candidates are qualified.
- Source: [HackerX](https://hackerx.org/employers/)

### Reverse job fair: candidates set up booths
Flips the traditional format: candidates set up booths with projects and portfolios, employers circulate. Puts candidates in active, confident position rather than supplicant mode.

**Adaptation:** AI safety career-changers each get a "station" with a project poster or demo. Org representatives and senior researchers circulate. Structured "warm intro" rounds where a facilitator introduces pairs with a sentence about why they should talk.
- Source: Various workforce development programs

### Recurse Center: career services as community infrastructure
Not a single event but a design philosophy:
- **"Never Graduate" culture:** Alumni are lifelong community members.
- **Career services are permanent:** Support forever, not just during batch. Dedicated team for placement, interview prep, introductions.
- **Company partnerships:** Places alumni, funded through recruiting fees.
- **Annual alumni week (Never Graduate Week):** 200+ alumni return for 5 days.

**Adaptation:** Build the event as the first in a recurring series. Create lightweight ongoing community (Slack, monthly check-ins). Track outcomes across events, not within a single one.
- Source: [RC career services](https://www.recurse.com/career-services)

### Fonzi AI: weekly match days
Replaced job boards with weekly "Match Days" for AI engineers. Curated, recurring, built on pre-vetted talent network. Recurring cadence keeps both sides engaged, reduces stakes of any single event.
- Source: [Fonzi AI](https://medium.com/fonzi-ai/we-replaced-job-boards-with-weekly-match-days-778753f5e36c)

### WomenHack: speed interviewing data
Data showing speed-interviewing formats significantly outperform traditional career fairs. Traditional model (wander booth to booth, collect swag) "designed for a labor market that no longer exists." Speed formats force real conversations.
- Source: [WomenHack](https://womenhack.com/tech-job-fairs-speed-interviewing-career-fairs/)

### Concrete format ideas for a 3-hour, 50-person AI safety career event

**Format A: "Safety Talent Demo Day" (YC-inspired)**
- Hour 1: 8-10 curated candidates give 3-min presentations on their work and goals. Audience includes org reps, senior researchers, hiring managers.
- Hour 2: Structured speed-matching (HackerX-style). Pre-matched pairs based on skills/interests get 8-min conversations.
- Hour 3: Open networking + "office hours" where senior people sit at tables by topic (technical, policy, ops, comms) and candidates rotate.

**Format B: "Reverse Safety Fair" (Reverse Job Fair + RC philosophy)**
- Candidates set up project stations (poster, demo, portfolio). Org reps and mentors circulate.
- Structured "warm intro" rounds where a facilitator explicitly introduces pairs.
- Final hour: facilitated small-group discussions by career stage ("exploring," "applying," "hiring").

**Key design principles:**
1. **Pre-vet both sides.** Screen candidates and orgs before the event. High signal requires curation.
2. **Structure the time.** Free-form networking favors extroverts and people who already know each other. Speed formats and facilitated intros equalize access.
3. **Track outcomes.** Follow up at 30/60/90 days. Did conversations lead to applications, interviews, offers, mentorships?
4. **Make it recurring.** One event produces anecdotes. A series produces a pipeline.
5. **Address the actual bottleneck.** The data says the gap is senior mentorship capacity. Consider a "mentor matching" component where senior people commit to 3 months of monthly check-ins with a matched junior, not just a single conversation.

## 7. Key Takeaways for Event Design

### From Apart Research
1. **Monthly cadence builds habit.** Consistent scheduling is how you grow from 100 to 766 signups.
2. **Co-hosting multiplies reach.** Each partner brings their audience. This is how Apart scaled.
3. **The hackathon-to-fellowship pipeline is the killer feature.** Prizes alone don't drive 766 signups. The Apart Lab fellowship (mentorship, publication, compute) gives serious participants a reason to return.
4. **Low barrier satellite model.** Central team handles intellectual infrastructure; local hosts just need a room and people.

### From ControlConf
5. **Hackathon-to-conference funnel works.** Run a hackathon a month before a conference, give the winner a funded trip, and you create progression from casual participation to deep engagement.
6. **Workshops for newcomers (day before) lower the barrier** without diluting the main event.

### From unconferences
7. **Unconference format works for established communities** with shared context. Good for a monthly event once the community is established, not for cold-start.
8. **Collaborative schedules** (some pre-planned + open slots) balance quality control with participant agency.

### From EAG events
9. **The core insight: curated 1-on-1s between people with complementary needs, not talks.** Pre-event profile sharing and intentional matching are the mechanism.
10. **For 50 people / 3 hours, skip Swapcard.** Use Luma for RSVP, Airtable for profiles + matching, structured time blocks for facilitated networking. EA North proves this works at your scale.
11. **Avoid the "too many meetings" trap.** At 3 hours, aim for 3-4 structured meetings plus unstructured time. EAG's exhaustion problem (10-15 meetings/day) is a real design failure.
12. **Solve the newcomer problem.** EAG's biggest failure mode: experienced attendees fill calendars early, newcomers feel lost. At 50 people monthly, solve with active host introductions and a "new attendee buddy" system.
13. **Track connections as your key metric**, not attendance. Target 4-6 meaningful new connections per person per event. Cost per connection is CEA's key metric too.

### From talent pipeline research
14. **The bottleneck is Stage 2: training to first role.** Plenty of interested people, but orgs need senior autonomous contributors. 10-30% of fellowship alumni cycle into another fellowship rather than converting to jobs.
15. **Senior mentorship capacity is the binding constraint.** Without enough senior people to mentor juniors, the whole pipeline backs up. An event that creates mentorship relationships (not just one-off conversations) would be uniquely valuable.
16. **"Publish your outputs" and "attend events and get to know people"** are the top two pieces of advice from AI safety org leaders (80,000 Hours survey). An event could explicitly support both.

### From career-matching format research
17. **Pre-vet both sides.** HackerX's success comes from curation. Everyone in the room should be there for a reason.
18. **Structure > free-form.** Speed formats and facilitated intros equalize access. Free-form networking favors extroverts and insiders.
19. **Give candidates the stage.** Reverse job fairs and talent demo days put candidates in an active position. A "3-minute project pitch" followed by structured matching is a proven model.
20. **Build recurring, not one-off.** Fonzi's weekly match days and RC's "never graduate" culture show that recurring cadence builds a pipeline. One event produces anecdotes; a series produces outcomes.
21. **Track 30/60/90 day outcomes.** Did conversations lead to applications, interviews, offers, mentorships? This is what separates a career event from a social.
